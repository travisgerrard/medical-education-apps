# When to Talk with a Clinician

![Patient having a supportive conversation with a clinician](/images/talk_clinician.png)

Many people wait too long to seek help for anxiety or depression—sometimes months or years. You don't need to wait until things get "bad enough" or reach a crisis to talk to a healthcare provider. Early intervention often leads to better and faster recovery.

## You Don't Need to Be "Sick Enough"

**Common thoughts that delay care**:
- "Other people have it worse"
- "I should be able to handle this on my own"
- "Maybe it will just get better"
- "I don't want to burden anyone"
- "I'm not sure it's 'real' depression/anxiety"

**The truth**: If your symptoms are affecting your life, that's reason enough to seek help. Depression and anxiety are medical conditions, not character flaws or signs of weakness.

## Clear Signs It's Time to Reach Out

### Persistent Symptoms

**When to act**: Symptoms have lasted **2 weeks or more** without improvement.

**What this looks like**:
- Feeling sad, empty, or hopeless most days
- Constant worrying or feeling on edge
- Lost interest in things you used to enjoy
- Changes in sleep (too much or too little)
- Significant changes in appetite or weight

### Daily Life Interference

**When to act**: Symptoms are making it hard to function in your daily life.

**What this looks like**:
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions at work/school
- Calling in sick frequently because you can't face the day
- Avoiding social situations or isolating from friends and family
- Struggling to complete basic self-care (showering, eating, paying bills)
- Neglecting responsibilities at home, work, or school

### Physical Symptoms

**When to act**: You're experiencing unexplained physical problems.

**What this looks like**:
- Persistent headaches, stomachaches, or body aches
- Chronic fatigue or exhaustion
- Frequent dizziness or nausea
- Chest pain or heart palpitations (after ruling out cardiac issues)
- Sleep problems that don't improve

**Note**: Always get physical symptoms checked by a doctor first to rule out medical causes.

### Coping in Unhealthy Ways

**When to act**: You're using substances or risky behaviors to manage your feelings.

**What this looks like**:
- Drinking alcohol daily or to excess
- Using drugs (prescription or otherwise) to cope
- Engaging in risky behaviors (reckless driving, unsafe sex)
- Self-harm (cutting, burning, hitting yourself)
- Binge eating or restricting food as a way to feel in control

### Relationship Strain

**When to act**: Your mood is affecting your relationships with people you care about.

**What this looks like**:
- Frequent conflicts with family or friends
- Withdrawing from loved ones
- Feeling irritable or snapping at people
- Loved ones expressing concern about you
- Difficulty maintaining relationships

### Work or School Problems

**When to act**: Your performance is declining.

**What this looks like**:
- Missing deadlines or struggling to complete tasks
- Unable to focus or remember things
- Attendance problems
- Receiving warnings or performance reviews
- Feeling overwhelmed by normal responsibilities

### Thoughts of Self-Harm or Suicide

**When to act**: **Immediately**. This is a mental health emergency.

**What this looks like**:
- Thoughts that you'd be better off dead
- Thoughts of hurting yourself
- Making plans for suicide
- Giving away belongings
- Saying goodbye to people

**Get help now**:
- Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline)
- Text "HELLO" to 741741 (Crisis Text Line)
- Go to your nearest emergency room
- Call 911

## Common Barriers (and Why They're Wrong)

### "I should be able to handle this myself"

**Reality**: Depression and anxiety are medical conditions that often require professional treatment. You wouldn't try to treat diabetes or a broken bone without medical help. Mental health is no different.

### "Therapy is for 'crazy' people"

**Reality**: About 1 in 5 adults experiences mental illness each year. Seeking help is a sign of strength and self-awareness, not weakness.

### "I can't afford it"

**Reality**: Many options exist:
- Insurance often covers mental health care (check your plan)
- Community mental health centers offer sliding-scale fees
- Some therapists offer reduced rates
- Online therapy can be more affordable
- Employee assistance programs (EAPs) often provide free sessions

### "I don't have time"

**Reality**: Untreated depression and anxiety will cost you more time in the long run through lost productivity, sick days, and suffering. One hour per week for therapy is an investment in getting your time back.

### "What if they judge me?"

**Reality**: Mental health professionals are trained to be non-judgmental. They've heard it all. Your struggles are valid, and their job is to help, not judge.

### "What if medication changes who I am?"

**Reality**: Medication doesn't change your personality. It helps your brain function better so you can be *more* like yourself, not less.

## Who to Contact First

### Primary Care Provider (PCP)

**Why start here**:
- Can screen for depression and anxiety
- Rule out medical causes (thyroid, vitamin deficiencies, etc.)
- Prescribe initial medication if needed
- Provide referrals to therapists or psychiatrists
- Often easier to schedule quickly

**Good for**: Initial screening, medical evaluation, mild to moderate symptoms.

### Therapist or Counselor

**Types**:
- **Psychologists** (PhD, PsyD): Can't prescribe medication, provide therapy
- **Licensed Clinical Social Workers** (LCSW): Provide therapy, can connect you to resources
- **Licensed Professional Counselors** (LPC): Provide therapy
- **Marriage and Family Therapists** (MFT): Specialize in relationship and family issues

**Good for**: Talk therapy, learning coping skills, working through trauma or life stressors.

### Psychiatrist

**What they do**: Medical doctors who specialize in mental health and can prescribe medication.

**Good for**: Moderate to severe symptoms, medication management, complex cases.

**Note**: Many psychiatrists focus on medication and may refer you to a therapist for talk therapy.

### Crisis Resources (for emergencies)

- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline**: Call or text 988
- **Crisis Text Line**: Text "HELLO" to 741741
- **Emergency Room**: For immediate safety concerns
- **Mobile Crisis Team**: Some areas have teams that come to you

## What to Expect at Your First Appointment

### The Screening Process

Your provider will likely:
1. Ask about your **symbols** (what you're experiencing)
2. Ask about **duration** (how long you've felt this way)
3. Ask about **severity** (how much symptoms interfere with your life)
4. Review your **medical history** and any medications
5. Ask about **family history** of mental health conditions
6. Rule out **physical causes** (may order blood work)

### Screening Tools You Might Complete

- **PHQ-9** (Patient Health Questionnaire-9): A 9-question assessment for depression
- **GAD-7** (Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7): A 7-question assessment for anxiety

These questionnaires help quantify severity and track progress, but they don't diagnose on their own.

### Questions You Might Be Asked

- "How long have you been feeling this way?"
- "Have you thought about hurting yourself?"
- "Are you sleeping more or less than usual?"
- "How is your appetite?"
- "Are you able to work/go to school?"
- "Do you have a support system?"
- "Have you tried anything that helps?"

**Be honest**. Providers can only help if they know what's really going on.

## How to Prepare for Your Appointment

### Before You Go

1. **Write down your symptoms**: List what you're experiencing and when it started
2. **Track your mood**: Note patterns (worse in mornings? Better on weekends?)
3. **List medications**: Include supplements and over-the-counter drugs
4. **Prepare questions**: What do you want to know about treatment options?
5. **Bring support**: It's okay to bring a trusted friend or family member

### Questions to Ask Your Provider

- "What do you think is causing my symptoms?"
- "What are my treatment options?"
- "How long will treatment take?"
- "What are the side effects of this medication?"
- "How will we know if treatment is working?"
- "What should I do if I feel worse?"
- "Can you recommend a therapist?"

### What If You Don't "Click" with the Provider?

It's okay to seek a second opinion or find a different provider. The relationship between you and your healthcare provider matters. If you don't feel heard or comfortable, it's okay to look for someone else.

## When to Seek a Higher Level of Care

**Outpatient therapy may not be enough if**:
- You have frequent thoughts of suicide
- You've made a suicide attempt
- You can't function at all (can't get out of bed, can't work)
- You're at risk of harming yourself or others
- You're experiencing psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusions)

**Options**:
- **Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)**: Several hours per day, 3-5 days/week
- **Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)**: Full days of treatment, return home at night
- **Inpatient Hospitalization**: 24/7 care in a hospital setting for severe cases

## The Bottom Line

**Seek help if**:
- Symptoms have lasted 2+ weeks
- Daily life is affected
- You're using unhealthy coping mechanisms
- Relationships are strained
- You're thinking about self-harm

**Don't wait if**:
- You're having suicidal thoughts (call 988 immediately)
- You're in crisis
- Symptoms are rapidly worsening

>
> Early intervention often leads to better and faster recovery. You don't need to wait until things are unbearable. If you're wondering whether you should seek help, that's often a sign that you should.

# Simple Equipment That Helps

Assistive devices and safety equipment can dramatically reduce fall risk. Many are inexpensive and don't require installation. Knowing what's available helps you choose the right tools for your needs.

## Bathroom Equipment

### Grab Bars
**Most important safety device:**
- Permanent installation ($15-40 per bar + installation)
- Suction-cup grab bars for renters ($20-35, less secure)
- Must support 250+ pounds

### Raised Toilet Seats
**Makes getting up easier:**
- Adds 3-6 inches of height ($25-60)
- Portable (no installation)
- With or without armrests
- Some attach securely, others just sit on top

### Toilet Safety Frames
**Armrests for support:**
- Freestanding around toilet ($40-100)
- Easy installation
- Adjustable height
- Folds for travel

### Shower and Tub Equipment
- **Shower chair**: Sit while bathing ($30-150)
- **Transfer bench**: Straddles tub edge ($40-80)
- **Hand-held shower head**: Control water flow ($20-100)
- **Non-slip mats**: Adhesive strips or suction mats ($10-25)
- **Bath lift**: Mechanically lowers you into tub ($200-500)

## Mobility Aids

### Canes
**When you need a cane:**
- Mild balance problems
- One-sided weakness
- Need occasional support

**Types:**
- **Standard single-point**: Most common ($15-30)
- **Quad cane**: Four-footed base, more stable ($25-45)
- **Offset handle**: Ergonomic grip ($20-35)

**Proper fit:**
- Handle at wrist height when standing
- Elbow bent 15-20 degrees
- Use on opposite side of weakness
- Replace worn rubber tip

### Walkers
**When you need a walker:**
- Moderate to severe balance problems
- Need support on both sides
- Recovering from surgery/injury

**Types:**
- **Standard walker**: Pick up and move ($30-60)
- **Front-wheeled walker**: Two front wheels ($40-70)
- **Four-wheeled rollator**: All wheels, has seat and brakes ($60-150)
- **Knee walker**: For foot/ankle injuries ($100-200)

**Choosing:**
- Rollators best for outdoor use
- Standard walkers most stable
- Must have hand brakes that work well
- Seat useful for resting

**Proper fit:**
- Handles at wrist height
- Arms bent 15-20 degrees
- Should stand upright, not lean forward

### Wheelchairs
**When mobility is very limited:**
- Can't walk safely even with walker
- Severe balance or strength problems
- Long distances only

**Types:**
- **Manual**: Self-propel or pushed ($100-500)
- **Transport chair**: Lightweight, must be pushed ($100-200)
- **Power**: Battery-operated ($1,000-5,000+)

## Home Safety Equipment

### Lighting
- **Motion-sensor nightlights**: Auto on/off ($8-15 each)
- **Touch lamps**: Easy to turn on ($15-30)
- **Smart bulbs**: Voice or phone control ($10-20/bulb)
- **Emergency light**: Turns on when power fails ($15-25)

### Reaching and Grabbing

![Simple tools like reachers and nightlights make daily life safer](/images/assistive_equipment_set_1763963652297.png)

- **Reacher/grabber**: Pick up items without bending ($10-25)
- **Sock aid**: Put on socks independently ($5-15)
- **Long-handled shoehorn**: Don't bend to put on shoes ($10-20)
- **Dressing stick**: Help with clothes ($10-15)

### Step Stools and Ladders
**If you must use them:**
- Step stool with handrail ($40-80)
- Non-slip surface
- Wide base
- **Better option**: Store items at accessible height

### Bed Safety
- **Bed rails**: Assist getting in/out ($30-80)
- **Bed cane/pole**: Leverage for standing ($40-70)
- **Bed risers**: Raise bed height ($15-30)

### Floor Safety
- **Non-slip rug pads**: If you must keep rugs ($10-20)
- **Contrast tape**: Mark step edges ($10-15)
- **Corner guards**: Protect from sharp edges ($5-10)

## Footwear

### Indoor
- **Non-slip slippers**: Rubber soles, secure fit ($15-40)
- **Diabetic shoes**: Extra support and cushioning ($50-100)
- **Walking shoes**: Athletic shoes indoors ($40-100)

**Avoid:**
- Socks alone
- Backless slippers
- Smooth-soled shoes
- Thongs/flip-flops

### Outdoor
- **Walking shoes**: Good tread, ankle support ($50-150)
- **Winter boots**: Non-slip sole, waterproof ($60-120)
- **Ice cleats**: Attach to shoes for ice ($15-30)

## Medical Alert Systems

**If living alone:**
- **Basic system**: Button to call for help ($25-45/month)
- **Fall detection**: Auto-calls if fall detected ($30-50/month)
- **GPS-enabled**: Works away from home ($35-55/month)
- **Two-way voice**: Speak to operator through device

**How they work:**
- Wear pendant or wristband
- Press button in emergency
- Connects to monitoring center
- They call family or 911

**Worth it if you:**
- Live alone
- Have high fall risk
- Worry about not being found
- Have fallen before

- **Light weights**: 1-5 lb dumbbells ($10-40)

- **Yoga mat**: Safe floor surface ($15-30)
- **Stretching strap**: Assistance with stretches ($8-15)

- **Bright LED flashlights**: Always handy ($10-25)

- **Pill organizers**: Daily/weekly doses ($5-15)

## How to Choose Equipment

### Questions to ask:
1. **What specific problem am I solving?**
   - Don't buy "just in case"
   - Address actual fall risks

2. **Does my insurance cover it?**
   - Medicare covers some items with prescription
   - Check before purchasing

3. **Can I try it first?**
   - Medical supply stores often have demos
   - Physical therapist can recommend

4. **Will I actually use it?**
   - Must be convenient
   - Not too complicated
   - Fits your lifestyle

### Where to Buy

**Medical supply stores:**
- Expert advice
- Try before buying
- More expensive

**Online:**
- Amazon, medical supply websites
- Better prices
- Can read reviews
- Can't test first

**Pharmacy chains:**
- Often carry basic items
- Moderate prices
- Limited selection

**Secondhand:**
- Thrift stores, Facebook Marketplace
- Very affordable
- Check safety carefully
- Replace rubber tips

## Medicare and Insurance Coverage

**Medicare Part B may cover:**
- Walkers
- Wheelchairs
- Hospital beds
- Canes (in some cases)

**Requirements:**
- Doctor's prescription stating "medically necessary"
- Purchase from Medicare-approved supplier
- Meet eligibility criteria

**Usually NOT covered:**
- Grab bars
- Raised toilet seats
- Shower chairs
- Over-the-counter items

**Check with:**
- Medicare: 1-800-MEDICARE
- Private insurance customer service
- Medical supply store billing department

## Professional Guidance

**Consult these professionals:**

**Physical Therapist:**
- Recommend specific equipment for your needs
- Train you on proper use
- Ensure correct sizing
- May provide prescription for insurance

**Occupational Therapist:**
- Home safety assessment
- Daily living aids
- Equipment modifications
- Training for adaptive equipment

**Your Doctor:**
- Write prescriptions for Medicare coverage
- Determine medical necessity
- Monitor your progress

## Safety Tips for Equipment Use

**Do:**
- Follow all instructions
- Check equipment regularly
- Replace worn parts
- Get training on proper use
- Keep equipment in good repair

**Don't:**
- Use damaged equipment
- Lend fitted equipment to others
- Modify equipment yourself
- Ignore discomfort (means wrong fit)

## Budget-Friendly Options

**Under $50:**
- Reacher/grabber
- Non-slip mats
- Nightlights
- Raised toilet seat
- Standard cane
- Sock aid

**$50-150:**
- Quad cane
- Shower chair
- Basic rollator
- Touch lamps
- Resistance bands

**Major investments ($150+):**
- Quality four-wheeled walker
- Medical alert system
- Grab bar installation
- Home lighting upgrades

## When Equipment Isn't Enough

**See your doctor if:**
- Equipment isn't preventing falls
- You need progressively more assistance
- You can't use equipment safely
- You've avoided activities despite equipment
- Falls continue to happen

## Remember

Equipment is a tool, not a cure. It works best when combined with:
- Exercise for strength and balance
- Home safety modifications
- Proper footwear
- Medication management
- Regular medical care

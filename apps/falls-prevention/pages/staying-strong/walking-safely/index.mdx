# Walking Safely

Walking is one of the best exercises for fall prevention—but only if done safety. This lesson covers how to walk confidently and avoid common hazards.

## Benefits of Walking

**For fall prevention:**
- Builds leg strength
- Improves balance and coordination
- Maintains bone density
- Enhances cardiovascular fitness
- Boosts mood and confidence

**Research shows**: Regular walking reduces fall risk by 20-30%

## Proper Walking Technique

### Posture

![Walk tall with head up and shoulders back](/images/walking_posture_guide_1763963851808.png)

- **Head up**: Look ahead, not down at feet
- **Shoulders back**: Avoid hunching forward
- **Chest out**: Open posture
- **Core engaged**: Slight abdominal tightening
- **Arms swing**: Naturally at sides

### Gait
- **Heel-to-toe**: Land on heel, roll through to toes
- **Normal stride**: Don't take tiny shuffling steps
- **Smooth movement**: Avoid jerky or stiff walking
- **Even pace**: Consistent speed

### Common Walking Mistakes
-Looking down constantly (affects balance)
- Shuffling feet (trip hazard)
- Leaning too far forward
- Taking steps that are too long or too short
- Walking too slowly (actually less stable)

## Indoor Walking Safety

### At Home
- Clear all walking paths
- Remove throw rugs
- Ensure good lighting
- Don't carry large items that block vision
- Wear proper footwear (not socks alone)
- Don't rush to answer phone or door

### Shopping/Public Buildings
- Use shopping cart for support if needed
- Avoid crowds when possible
- Watch for wet floors
- Take your time on escalators/moving walkways
- Rest when tired

## Outdoor Walking Safety

### Choosing Routes
- Start with familiar, flat areas
- Avoid uneven sidewalks initially
- Stay in well-lit areas
- Walking during daylight hours
- Routes with benches for resting

### Weather Considerations

**Rain:**
- Wet surfaces extremely slippery
- Reduced visibility
- Consider indoor walking instead
- If must go out, slow down significantly

**Ice and Snow:**
- Most dangerous conditions
- Ice cleats on shoes ($15-30)
- Walk like a penguin (small steps, arms out)
- **Best option**: Indoor walking until clear

**Hot Weather:**
- Stay hydrated
- Walk during cooler parts of day
- Watch for dizziness/fatigue
- Can affect blood pressure medications

### Surface Awareness
- **Concrete**: Generally safe, watch for cracks
- **Asphalt**: Can be uneven, affected by weather
- **Grass**: Uneven, hides obstacles
- **Gravel**: Unstable, difficult balance
- **Sand**: Very challenging balance

## Walking aids

### When to Use
- Feel unsteady
- Have balance problems
- Recovering from injury
- Long distances
- Unfamiliar terrain

### Types

**Cane:**
- Mild balance issues
- Use on opposite side of weakness
- Proper height critical

**Walker:**
- Moderate to severe balance problems
- More stable than cane
- Rollator best for outdoor use

**Using Aids Correctly:**
- Professional fitting essential
- Learn proper technique
- Check rubber tips regularly
- Don't be embarrassed—they enable independence

## Walking Speed

**Too slow = less stable:**
- Need certain speed for balance
- Momentum helps
- Don't creep along

**Too fast = dangerous:**
- Can't react to hazards
- More forceful fall if you trip

**Just right = comfortable pace:**
-Normal conversation possible
- Not rushed
- Steady, even steps

## Dual-Task Walking

**The problem:**
- Walking while doing other tasks is harder
- Talking, carrying, looking around
- Increases fall risk significantly

**Safer approach:**
- Stop walking to have conversation
- Put packages down to open doors
- Stand still to look at phone
- Single-task when on stairs or uneven ground

## Footwear for Walking

**Best choices:**
- Athletic/walking shoes
- Low, wide heel
- Non-slip rubber sole
- Laces or velcro (secure fit)
- Good arch support

**Avoid:**
- High heels
- Backless shoes/slippers
- Smooth leather soles
- Shoes that are too big
- Flip-flops
- Platform shoes

## Building a Walking Program

**Week 1-2:**
- 10 minutes daily
- Flat, familiar routes
- Indoor or safe outdoor

**Week 3-4:**
- 15-20 minutes
- Begin slight variation in route
- Practice turning, stopping

**Week 5+:**
- Work toward 30 minutes
- More challenging routes
- Different surfaces (safely)

### Sample Walking Routine
- 5 min warm-up (slow pace)
- 20 min moderate pace
- 5 min cool-down (slow pace)
- Light stretching after

## Walking Indoors

**Home:**
- Up and down hallways
- Around rooms in circuit
- Stairs (if safe)

**Shopping malls:**
- Climate controlled
- Flat, smooth surface
- Other walkers
- Benches available
- Open before stores (mall walking groups)

**Community centers:**
- Indoor tracks
- Walking groups
- Supervised
- Social aspect

## Walking with Others

**Benefits:**
- Motivation and accountability
- Safety (someone nearby if you fall)
- Social interaction
- More enjoyable

**Group walking:**
- Senior center programs
- Neighborhood groups
- Mall walking clubs
- YMCA programs

## Monitoring Intensity

**Talk test:**
- Should be able to talk but slightly breathless
- If can't talk, too hard
- If can sing, too easy

**Perceived exertion:**
- Moderate: noticeable effort but not exhausting
- Can continue 20-30 minutes
- Feel worked but not wiped out

## Obstacles and Hazards

**Common outdoor hazards:**
- Curbs (use curb cuts, watch height differences)
- Cracks in sidewalk
- Wet leaves(slip risk)
- Pets off leash
- Uneven pavement
- Protruding roots

**Avoiding trips:**
- Lift feet completely
- Don't drag toes
- Watch path ahead
- Step over, not on, obstacles

## When to Stop and Rest

**Warning signs:**
- Shortness of breath
- Chest discomfort
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Leg pain or cramping
- Excessive fatigue

**Smart strategy:**
- Walk where benches available
- Don't push through concerning symptoms
- Build endurance gradually

## Night Walking

**Generally avoid unless necessary:**
- Vision worse in dark
- Harder to see obstacles
- Depth perception impaired

**If you must:**
- Well-lit areas only
- Reflective clothing
- Flashlight
- Walk with partner

## Using Treadmill

**Advantages:**
- Controlled environment
- Flat, even surface
- Can hold handrails
- Weather independent

**Safety tips:**
- Start very slow
- Use safety clip
- Don't look down at feet
- Hold handrails if needed
- Step off to sides, not backwards

## Walking Goals

**For fall prevention:**
- Minimum: 20-30 minutes, 5 days/week
- Optimal: 30-40 minutes, most days
- Include some variety (speeds, surfaces)

**Doesn't have to be continuous:**
- Three 10-minute walks = one 30-minute walk
- Break it up if needed
- Consistency more important than duration

## Progression

**Increase gradually:**
- No more than 10% increase per week
- Duration before intensity
- Surface variation last

**Example progression:**
- Month 1: 15 min, flat, smooth surface
- Month 2: 20 min, same surface
- Month 3: 25 min, slight variations
- Month 4: 30 min, more variety

## Staying Motivated

**Strategies:**
- Walk with friend
- Track steps (pedometer/phone)
- Set small goals
- Vary routes
- Listen to music/audiobooks
- Join walking group

**Tracking:**
- Use step counter
- Log walks
- Note how you feel
- Celebrate milestones

## Walking in Different Settings

**Urban:**
- Busy sidewalks
- Traffic to navigate
- More obstacles
- Choose less crowded times

**Suburban:**
- Neighborhoods
- Generally quieter
- Watch for driveways
- Uneven sidewalks

**Rural:**
- Road shoulders
- Uneven terrain
- Less traffic but faster speeds
- Reflective clothing essential

## Emergency Preparedness

**Always carry:**
- ID
- Phone
- Medical alert information
- Emergency contact numbers

**Tell someone:**
- Your route
- Expected return time
- Check in after walk

## Remember

Walking is simple, free, and one of the best things you can do for fall prevention. But safe walking requires attention and the right approach.

Don't let fear stop you from walking—use smart strategies to walk safely. Each walk builds strength, balance, and confidence.

Start where you are, even if that's just down the driveway. Every step counts toward preventing falls and maintaining independence.

The goal isn't to walk fast or far—it's to walk safely and regularly.
